Level 01 — Storytelling
01
Graphic Novel
Your Original Story
30–45 min
›

Yesterday you made a Spider-Man graphic novel. Today: no adaptations. You're going to invent something. It doesn't have to be long or complex — the best ideas are one sentence.

Your Mission
Come up with ONE original character. Give them:
→ A name
→ One unusual power or skill
→ One problem they can't solve with that power

Then tell Claude Code: "Create a graphic novel about [your idea]"
💡
Power move: After the first version is generated, look at it critically. What's one page you wish was different? Tell Claude to fix exactly that one thing. Iteration is the skill.
// example character ideas

Here are 3 made-up characters to show you the format. You can use one of these OR make your own — but try to make your own first.

Example A

Mira the Hollow
Power: She can walk through walls and become invisible.
Problem: She's been invisible so long, people she loves can no longer see her — even when she tries to be seen.

Example B

Kael the Burnkeeper
Power: He can absorb fire and store it inside his body.
Problem: The fire is slowly burning him from the inside — and he has to keep taking in more to survive.

Example C

Sona the Echo
Power: She can copy any skill she watches someone do once.
Problem: Every skill she copies erases one of her own memories. She's getting stronger — but forgetting who she is.

Notice the pattern: the power sounds cool, but the problem comes from the power itself. That's what makes a great character.

Prompt to Claude Code

Create a graphic novel about Kael the Burnkeeper — a boy who can absorb fire but it's slowly destroying him from the inside. Dark, gritty art style, 8 pages, with a cliffhanger ending.

  1. Spend 5 minutes writing your character idea on paper first (no AI yet)
  2. Open Claude Code. Give it your one-line idea.
  3. Review the result. Find one thing to improve.
  4. Give Claude a specific instruction to fix it.
  5. Save the final version.

Level 02 — Image Generation
02
Image Generation
The Prompt Ladder
20 min
›

Prompting is a skill. A vague prompt gets a generic image. A precise prompt gets exactly what you imagined. This mission trains that muscle.

The Challenge
Pick one scene from The Hobbit or Percy Jackson.

Round 1 — Short prompt (5 words max). Generate it.
Round 2 — Add details: lighting, weather, mood, camera angle.
Round 3 — Add a style: "oil painting", "manga", "photorealistic", "concept art".

Compare all three. What changed?
  1. Pick your scene. Write it in one sentence.
  2. Generate Round 1 with the shortest possible prompt.
  3. Generate Round 2 — add 2–3 details.
  4. Generate Round 3 — add an art style.
  5. Write down: what word made the biggest difference?
🎯
The insight: Every word in a prompt is a vote. More specific words win more votes. "A warrior" vs "a scarred Viking warrior at sunset, tired eyes, holding a cracked shield" — same character, completely different image.
// prompt ladder — bilbo baggins escaping the goblins

Here's what the same scene looks like at each round. Watch how much the image changes.

Round 1 — too vague

A hobbit running from goblins

Round 2 — getting specific

A small barefoot hobbit sprinting through a dark underground tunnel, goblins chasing behind him with torches, dramatic side lighting, panic on his face

Round 3 — locked in

A small barefoot hobbit sprinting through a dark underground tunnel, goblins chasing behind him with torches, dramatic side lighting, panic on his face, dark fantasy oil painting style, deep shadows, flickering orange firelight, cinematic composition

Try your own scene — maybe Perseus fighting Medusa, or the Shire on a rainy morning, or Gandalf arriving at Bilbo's door. Any scene you can picture clearly works.

Style options to try

oil painting · manga · watercolor · photorealistic · pencil sketch · concept art · storybook illustration · film noir

Detail words that help

golden hour · stormy · backlit · close-up · aerial view · motion blur · fog · dramatic shadows · high contrast

Level 03 — Motion & Video
03
AI Video
The 5-Second Shot
25 min
›

Video is harder than images because it involves time. You have to describe not just what's in the frame, but what moves, how it moves, and what the camera does.

Your Mission
Write a "shot brief" for a 5-second video clip.

It must include:
→ What's in the frame (subject)
→ What's moving, and how (action)
→ Camera movement (static / zoom / pan / orbit)
→ Lighting / time of day
→ Mood in one word

Then generate it with Kling or Veo 3.
🎥
Director's note: Real film directors write exactly this for every shot before filming. You're learning a professional skill. Try: a shot from your graphic novel character's story.
// example shot brief → prompt

Here's a complete shot brief and the final prompt it turns into. Use this exact format for yours.

Shot Brief (written on paper)

Subject: A young warrior standing on a cliff edge
Action: His cape whips violently in the wind, he doesn't move
Camera: Starts far away, slowly zooms in toward his back
Lighting: Sunset, deep orange sky, long shadows
Mood: Determination

Final Prompt (paste into Kling)

A young warrior in dark armor stands motionless on a cliff edge. His cape thrashes in powerful wind. The camera begins wide and slowly pushes in toward his silhouette from behind. Dramatic sunset sky, deep orange and red, long shadows across the rocks. Slow and cinematic. Mood: quiet determination before a battle.

Notice: the brief uses short notes. The prompt turns those notes into full sentences. Claude Chat can help you convert a brief into a prompt if you get stuck — just paste your brief and ask.

More scene ideas if you're stuck

A dragon landing on a mountain peak at dawn · A character discovering a hidden door in a forest · A crowded market suddenly going silent · A spaceship emerging from clouds over a city · Two characters facing each other before a fight

  1. Write your shot brief on paper first (don't skip this)
  2. Convert it into a single prompt paragraph
  3. Generate the clip
  4. Watch it. What's one thing the AI got wrong?
  5. Rewrite that part of the brief and regenerate

Level 04 — Critical Thinking
04
AI Analysis
Break the AI
15 min
›

The best AI users aren't the ones who generate the most — they're the ones who can spot what's wrong and fix it. This mission trains your critical eye.

Your Mission
Take any AI output from today (a page from your graphic novel, an image, or a video clip).

Write 3 things that are wrong or could be better — be specific.
Then: for each problem, write the exact instruction you'd give to fix it.

You don't have to actually fix them all. Just prove you can see them.
🔍
The key question: "Does this match what I imagined?" If the answer is no — where exactly does it break down? Hands? Lighting? Proportions? The more precisely you can name the problem, the more precisely you can fix it.
// example: spotting problems and writing fixes

Say you generated an image of your warrior character on a cliff. Here's what a good critique looks like vs a bad one.

Bad critique (too vague)

Problem 1: It doesn't look right
Problem 2: The character is off
Problem 3: I don't like the background

Fix: Make it better

Good critique (specific + fixable)

Problem 1: The character's hands look melted together
Fix: "Regenerate with the character's hands clearly separated, one holding a sword at his side"

Problem 2: The sky is blue but I wanted sunset
Fix: "Change the sky to deep orange and red sunset, with the sun low on the horizon"

Problem 3: The cliff looks fake and flat
Fix: "Make the cliff rocky and jagged, with more depth and texture"

The rule: if you can't describe the problem in one specific sentence, you haven't looked closely enough yet. Zoom in. What exactly is wrong?

  1. Pick any output from today
  2. Write: Problem 1, Problem 2, Problem 3 (be very specific)
  3. For each: write the instruction you'd give Claude to fix it
  4. Try fixing at least one of them
Level 05 — Boss Challenge
05
Final Challenge
The Trailer
Open-ended
›

Combine everything. You're going to make a mini trailer for your original graphic novel character from Mission 01. This doesn't have to be long — even 3 clips and a title card is a trailer.

What You Need
Shot 1 — Establishing: who is your character, where do they live?
Shot 2 — The problem: show what they're up against
Shot 3 — The action: one powerful moment
Title card — Character name + one tagline

Ask Claude Chat to help you write the shot briefs if you get stuck.
🏆
This is real: What you're building here is basically what a professional would do in a pitch meeting. "Here's my character, here's their world, here's why you should care." The tools will get better — this skill stays.
// example trailer — kael the burnkeeper

Here's a complete 3-shot trailer plan for the example character from Mission 1. Use this as your template.

Shot 1 — Establishing (Who + Where)

A teenage boy walks alone through the ruins of a burned village at dawn. Ash floats through cold air. He stops and stares at a charred house — his hands glow faintly orange. Camera slowly orbits around him. Mood: lonely, haunted.

Shot 2 — The Problem (What's At Stake)

Close-up of Kael's chest — cracks of orange fire glow through his skin, spreading. He grabs his own shirt, breathing hard. Cut to black. A voice whispers: "How much longer can you hold it?" Mood: dread.

Shot 3 — The Action (The Hook)

Kael stands in front of a massive wall of fire. Instead of running, he opens his arms and absorbs the entire blaze in one burst — his body blazing white. He screams. Then silence. He's still standing. Mood: terrifying power.

Title Card

KAEL THE BURNKEEPER

Tagline: "The fire doesn't burn him. It lives inside him."

Now do the same for your character from Mission 1. If you want help writing the shot briefs, paste this template into Claude Chat and ask it to help you fill it in for your character.
