The Printer's Rule

Test the joint before the full print. A full katana takes over an hour to print. A joint test piece takes 15 minutes. If the tang doesn't fit the socket, you want to find out in 15 minutes, not 90. Always test the hard part first.

Every 3D print starts with a question: how should this piece sit on the print bed? The answer matters more than any other setting. A piece oriented wrong will warp, snap, or need supports that ruin the surface.

Ask Claude Code to figure out the right orientation for each piece:

💬 Say this to Claude Code
I'm ready to print my katana. I have three STL files: katana-blade.stl, katana-guard.stl, katana-handle.stl. My printer is a Qidi Q1 Pro (245x245x245mm build volume). For each piece, tell me:

1. How should it sit on the print bed? (which face down, which direction)
2. What slicer settings should I use? (layer height, wall count, infill %, whether I need a brim or supports)
3. Why that orientation? (what goes wrong if I orient it differently)

Give me step-by-step instructions for setting each one up in Qidi Studio.

Here's what Claude Code should tell you — and why each piece is different:

Blade
Flat on its broad face
The blade is long and thin. Lying flat means layers stack through the thin dimension — much stronger against snapping. Add a 5mm brim to prevent the edges from curling up.
Guard
Flat on the disc face
It's a flat disc — just lay it down. Only ~30 layers tall. The tang hole prints straight through. Easiest piece.
Handle
Standing upright, socket facing up
The socket (blind hole on top) prints cleanly this way — no supports needed. The octagonal base sits flat on the bed.

Key slicer settings for the katana:

Setting Blade Guard Handle
Layer height 0.15mm 0.20mm 0.20mm
Walls 4 3 3
Infill 80% 40% 40%
Brim 5mm No No
Supports None None None
Est. time ~45 min ~12 min ~30 min
💡
Why 4 walls on the blade? The blade is only ~5mm thick. With 4 walls at 0.4mm each, that's 1.6mm of solid wall per side — 3.2mm total out of 5mm. The blade is almost entirely solid. That's what makes it strong enough to handle without snapping.
💡
Why 0.15mm layers on the blade? Finer layers give smoother surfaces on the curved sori. The guard and handle don't need this — 0.20mm is fine for flat surfaces and cylinders.

  2. Orient each piece using Claude Code's instructions.
  3. Set the slicer settings for each piece (or ask Claude Code to set up a profile).
  4. Don't print yet — you're testing the joint first.

This is the mission that saves you from wasting an hour. You'll print just the joint area — a tiny section of each piece where they connect. It takes about 15 minutes to print. If the tang doesn't fit the slot, you find out now.

💬 Say this to Claude Code
I need a joint test print before I commit to the full katana. Take my katana model and export three small test pieces: the last 20mm of the blade (including the full tang), the guard, and the top 20mm of the handle (including the socket). Export these as separate STL files I can load into the slicer. Use the same slicer settings as the full pieces.
  1. Load the test pieces into Qidi Studio. Same orientations as the full pieces.
  2. Slice and print. All three test pieces should fit on the bed at once — total print time ~15 minutes.
  3. When the print finishes, remove the pieces and try assembling them by hand.

The moment of truth. Try sliding the tang through the guard slot and into the handle socket. What happens?

  • ✅ Slides in with a little resistance, holds snug: Perfect. Your clearances are right. Print the full pieces.
  • 🔨 Too tight, won't go in: Sand the tang lightly with 150-grit sandpaper. Or tell Claude Code: "The tang is too tight for the slot. Increase clearance by 0.1mm per side, re-export, and I'll reprint the test."
  • 🔄 Too loose, rattles around: Tell Claude Code: "The tang is too loose in the slot. Decrease clearance by 0.1mm per side, re-export, and I'll reprint the test." Or: wrap one layer of masking tape around the tang for a quick fix.
  • ❌ Shapes don't match at all: Something went wrong in the export. Tell Claude Code exactly what you see. Show it a photo if you can.
⚠️
Elephant's foot. The very first layer of a print often squishes out slightly wider than designed. This can make the guard's tang hole too tight at the bottom. If the tang goes in most of the way but catches at one end, this is probably why. Light sanding or a needle file fixes it in seconds.
🏆
15 minutes to validate what took an hour to design. This is the engineering discipline that separates a successful build from a frustrating one. Professional engineers call this a "fit check" — they do it every time, even on parts they've built before.

Your joint test passed. The clearances work. Now print the real pieces.

💬 Say this to Claude Code
Joint test passed — the tang fits through the guard and into the handle. I'm ready to print the full pieces. Remind me of the slicer settings and orientation for each piece. Should I print them one at a time or all at once?
Printing one at a time is safer (less stringing, less risk of a failure ruining all pieces). But all three fit on the bed at once if you want to save time.

Print order recommendation:

  1. Guard first (~12 min). It's the fastest and simplest. Quick win while you wait for the bigger pieces.
  2. Handle second (~30 min). Medium print, straightforward.
  3. Blade last (~45 min). Longest print, finest layers. Keep an eye on it for the first few layers — if the brim sticks well, you're good.

While the printer runs, you wait. That's normal — 3D printing is a slow craft. Come back when it's done.

Assembly.

All three pieces printed. Now you put them together.

  1. Clean up the blade. If you used a brim, peel or cut it off. A fingernail works for PLA. Trim any rough edges with a hobby knife.
  2. Test-fit the tang through the guard. It should slide through the slot just like the test piece. If it catches, light sanding on the tang.
  3. Slide the tang into the handle socket. Push it all the way in until the guard sits snug between the blade base and the handle top.
  4. Check the fit. Hold the handle and give it a gentle shake. Does the blade wobble? If yes, a drop of super glue in the socket will lock it. If the fit is snug, you might not need glue at all.
// common assembly issues
Tang too tight

Wrap the tang with 150-grit sandpaper and rotate back and forth. Remove a tiny bit at a time. Test after every 10 seconds of sanding. You're only removing fractions of a millimeter.

Tang too loose

Wrap one layer of masking tape around the tang where it sits inside the guard or handle. Or: a small drop of medium super glue (CA glue) in the socket before inserting. Hold for 60 seconds.

Guard hole tight on one side

Elephant's foot — the first layer squished wider. Use a small needle file or folded sandpaper to open up the tight edge. Only file the entry side.

Blade tip broke

Thin tips are fragile in PLA. If it snapped, you can glue it back with super glue, or tell Claude Code to make the tip slightly thicker (at least 2mm) and reprint just the blade.

The permanent fix

If the fit is good but you want it locked forever: one drop of medium CA (super glue) in the handle socket, slide the tang in, hold 60 seconds. Do NOT glue the guard — friction fit only, so you can take it apart if needed.
