The Game Designer's Rule

A game designer at a studio doesn't write every line of code or draw every sprite. They design the experience — what the player sees, what they do, what surprises them, what makes them want to play "one more round." Your job today is to be the game designer. Claude Code is your dev team.

Level 01 — Game Design
01
Planning
The Design Doc
10 min
›

Before you touch Claude Code, you need to know what you're building. Professional game designers write a "design doc" — a short description of the game that anyone on the team can read and understand immediately.

Your Mission
Write a design doc in 3 lines:

→ What does the player see when the game starts?
→ What does the player do? (click, choose, explore, fight, solve?)
→ How does the player win — or lose?

Pick a type of game that excites you — or invent your own.

🗡️
Dungeon Crawler
Explore rooms on a map. Find items. Fight monsters. Reach the final boss. Turn-based, every choice matters.
🌎
Survival
You're stranded somewhere. Manage food, health, shelter. Make hard decisions. How many days can you last?
🔍
Mystery
A crime happened. Search a scene, question suspects, collect clues, piece together what happened before time runs out.
⚔️
Battle Arena
Turn-based fights. Pick your attacks, manage health and mana. Defeat increasingly tough enemies to become champion.
🚀
Space Explorer
Your ship. Your crew. Visit planets, each with different challenges and discoveries. Map the unknown galaxy.
💡
Your Own Idea
Card game, tower defense, tycoon, escape room, trivia — if you can describe it clearly, Claude Code can build it.
⚠️
Start small. The best first games are ONE screen, ONE mechanic, ONE goal. "Explore a 5-room dungeon and find the key" is a great game. "Open-world RPG with 50 quests" is a disaster. You can always add more rooms, enemies, and features AFTER the basic game works.
// example design docs — 3 lines each

A design doc doesn't have to be long. Three lines is enough. Here are three examples at different difficulty levels.

Dungeon Crawler

See: A dark room with 3 doors. Your stats (health, gold, items) on the side.

Do: Pick a door. Each leads to a room with a monster, a treasure, or a trap. Turn-based combat: choose attack, defend, or run.

Win/Lose: Find the golden key on floor 5. Lose if health hits 0.

Survival

See: A forest clearing. Day counter, hunger bar, health bar, inventory.

Do: Each "day" you pick one action: explore, hunt, build shelter, rest. Random events happen. Resources go up and down.

Win/Lose: Survive 10 days until rescue arrives. Lose if hunger or health hits 0.

Mystery

See: A detective's desk with a case file. Three locations to visit, a clue board, a suspect list.

Do: Visit locations to find clues. Question suspects. Drag clues onto the board to connect them. When you think you know who did it, make your accusation.

Win/Lose: Accuse the right person with enough evidence. Only 3 chances — accuse wrong 3 times and the case goes cold.

Pick one of these or write your own. The format is always the same: See / Do / Win-Lose.

  1. Look at the game types. Which one makes you want to play it?
  2. Write your 3-line design doc: See, Do, Win/Lose
  3. Read it back. Could someone who's never talked to you understand the game? If not, make it clearer.

Build
First Playable
25 min
›

This is the big one. You're going to hand Claude Code your design doc and tell it to build a working, playable game. The first version won't be perfect — in the industry they call this the "first playable." Your job is to play it and figure out what needs to change.

💬 Say this to Claude Code
Build me a game as an HTML file I can play in my browser. Here's the concept:

[PASTE YOUR 3-LINE DESIGN DOC]

Make it look good — dark theme, clean UI, satisfying to interact with. It should feel like a real game, not a homework assignment. Use animations and transitions to make actions feel responsive.
Replace the bracketed section with your actual design doc from Mission 1.
⏳
Claude Code will write a LOT of code. HTML, CSS, JavaScript — potentially hundreds of lines. This might take a minute or two. Let it work. When it's done, open the file in your browser and actually play the game. Don't just look at it — click every button, try to win, try to lose, try to break it.
🎯
The #1 mistake: Asking for 10 changes at once. Fix one thing at a time. Play between each fix. If you stack 10 requests, you'll get 10 mediocre changes. If you fix one, play, fix one, play — each change will be exactly right.
// example — building a dungeon crawler
Round 1 — First Playable

You: Build me a game as an HTML file. Concept: dark room with 3 doors, each leads to a room with a monster/treasure/trap, turn-based combat (attack/defend/run), find the golden key on floor 5, lose if health hits 0. Dark theme, make it feel like a real game.

Claude Code: [writes ~400 lines of code, builds the whole game]

Round 2 — First Fix

You (after playing): Combat is too easy — I never take damage. Make the monsters actually dangerous. The skeleton should hit hard, and the troll should be terrifying.

Claude Code: [adjusts monster stats, adds damage variation]

Round 3 — Adding Juice

You: Way better. But when I open a door, it's instant — boring. I want a moment of darkness before revealing what's inside. And when I take damage, make the screen shake a little.

Claude Code: [adds door-opening animation, screen shake on damage]

That's the loop: play → spot the problem → describe the fix → play again. Every round the game gets better because you directed the change.

  1. Open Claude Code. Paste your design doc with the build instructions.
  2. Open the HTML file it creates. Play the whole game — try to win AND try to lose.
  3. What's fun? What's boring? What's broken? Pick the biggest problem.
  4. Tell Claude Code ONE specific thing to fix. Play again.
  5. Do at least 2-3 rounds of this. Each round the game gets better.

Level 03 — Art Direction
03
Visuals
The Art Pass
15 min
›

Your game works. But right now it probably looks like boxes and text. Time to give it a visual identity. You're going to direct Claude Code to generate custom art with Nano Banana and embed it directly into your game.

Your Mission
Tell Claude Code to generate at least 3 images for your game:

→ A title screen — the first thing the player sees
→ A key scene — a character, monster, location, or item
→ An atmosphere image — a background, environment, or mood shot

The images should be embedded in the game, not separate files.
💬 Say something like this
Now make my game look amazing. Use Nano Banana to generate images for:

1. A title screen showing [describe the scene/mood]
2. [A character portrait / an enemy / a key item]
3. [A background / environment for the main scene]

Embed them directly into the game HTML. Art style: [dark fantasy / pixel art / sci-fi / painted / etc].
Be specific about the art style. It changes everything — you know this from Day 2.
🎨
The style sets the world. "Pixel art retro" makes it feel like a classic arcade game. "Dark painted concept art" makes it feel like a AAA title. "Clean vector illustration" makes it feel like a polished mobile game. You're not just picking art — you're picking the feeling of your entire game.
// example — art for a dungeon crawler
Title Screen

"A massive stone dungeon entrance carved into a mountainside, torches flickering on either side, dark fog rolling out from inside. Text space at the top. Dark fantasy oil painting style, dramatic lighting."

Art styles that work well for games

pixel art 16-bit · dark fantasy oil painting · anime/manga · sci-fi concept art · watercolor storybook · comic book · low-poly 3D render · noir black and white · retro movie poster · Studio Ghibli

  1. Decide: what art style fits your game?
  2. List 3+ images your game needs (title, characters, environments).
  3. Tell Claude Code to generate them with Nano Banana and embed them.
  4. Play the game again. Does the art make it feel more real?
  5. If an image doesn't match your vision, describe what's wrong and regenerate just that one.

Level 04 — Playtesting
04
Playtest
The Real Test
15 min
›

Here's a secret from the game industry: the developer is the worst person to test their own game. You already know where everything is, what to click, how to win. You need fresh eyes.

Your Mission
Part 1 — Play your game one final time. Find 3 things to improve. Tell Claude Code to fix them.

Part 2 — Hand your game to someone else. Watch them play. Don't help. Don't explain. Just watch.

When you watch someone else play, look for these signals:

  • 😕 Where do they get confused? That means the instructions aren't clear enough. The game should explain itself.
  • 🥱 Where do they get bored? That means it's too easy or too slow. The game needs more tension.
  • 😡 Where do they get frustrated? That means it's too hard or feels unfair. The game needs better balance.
  • 😁 Where do they smile or laugh? That means it's working. Figure out why and do more of that.
💭
The hardest part: When they ask "what do I do?" — don't answer. That urge to explain means the game isn't clear enough yet. That's not the player's fault. Tell Claude Code to add better instructions, a tutorial moment, or visual cues so the NEXT player won't need to ask.
// example — watching your brother play
What you observe

He opens the game and stares at the screen for 5 seconds. He doesn't know he's supposed to click a door. He clicks randomly, finds a door, fights a monster — but doesn't realize he can use "defend" to block damage. He dies on floor 2 and says "that's unfair."

What you tell Claude Code

"Three fixes: (1) When the game starts, add a short message like 'Click a door to begin exploring' that fades after 3 seconds. (2) In the first combat, add a hint that says 'Try using Defend — it blocks half the damage.' (3) The monster on floor 2 hits too hard for that early — reduce its damage by 30%."

Every one of those fixes came from watching someone struggle, not from playing it yourself. That's why playtesting with fresh eyes is so powerful.

  1. Play your game. List 3 things to improve — be specific.
  2. Tell Claude Code to fix them. Play one more time to confirm.
  3. Give the game to someone else. Watch them play without saying a word.
  4. Write down what confused them, what they enjoyed, and what felt unfair.
  5. (Optional) Make one last round of fixes based on what you observed.
