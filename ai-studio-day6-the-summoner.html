The Summoner's Rule

The AI builds what you describe, not what you imagine. The gap between those two things is the skill. Every detail you name is a detail the AI adds. Anything you leave out, the AI guesses. Your prompt is the blueprint.

Pick something. Describe it. The AI sculpts it.

This is a different kind of AI than what you used for images or code. This one takes your words and builds a full 3D object — geometry you can rotate, zoom into, and eventually print. The catch: you get one shot per generation. You can't say "make the horns bigger." You have to describe the whole thing, every time. So your description is everything.

Pick what you want to summon:

🦖
Dinosaur Skull
T-Rex, raptor, triceratops — on a museum display base. Bone texture, teeth, cracks, weathering. Like something you'd see behind glass.
🐉
Dragon
Full dragon coiled on a rocky base, or a dragon head bust. Wings tucked close to the body. Scales, horns, glowing-eye detail.
🎮
Your Game Character
A character from your game, your comics, or your imagination. Standing hero pose on a round base. This is YOUR creation — make it real.
🤖
Mech Warrior
Battle robot. Armored suit. Giant mech with heavy weapons. Chunky proportions, standing on a platform. Think Iron Giant, not stick figure.
🔨
Legendary Weapon
Thor's hammer on a stone pedestal. A wizard's staff. A cursed sword in an anvil. Simple shape, maximum detail — prints clean.
💡
Your Own Invention
Something that doesn't exist yet. Alien creature. Hybrid animal. A weapon from a world you made up. The weirder the description, the more interesting the result.

Before you type anything — write your description on paper first. Not in Claude Code. On paper. This forces you to think about what you actually want before the clock starts.

// prompt craft — the difference detail makes
Weak — vague

A dinosaur skull

Strong — specific

A Tyrannosaurus Rex skull with jaws open showing rows of sharp teeth, deep eye sockets, and cracked bone texture. Weathered and ancient-looking. Mounted on a rectangular stone museum pedestal. Single solid object.

The weak prompt gives the AI nothing to work with. It will produce something generic. The strong prompt names specific details — open jaws, teeth, eye sockets, bone texture, weathering. Every detail you name is a detail the AI adds. The phrase "mounted on a stone pedestal" also solves a printing problem: it gives the model a flat bottom.

Weak

A robot

Strong

A heavy battle mech with thick armored legs, a cockpit head with a glowing visor, and a cannon mounted on the right shoulder. Battle-damaged with scratches and scorch marks. Standing on a metal platform base. Chunky proportions, single solid piece.

Notice "chunky proportions, single solid piece." This tells the AI to keep parts thick and connected — important for 3D printing. Without it, you might get thin antenna or weapons that snap off, or parts floating in mid-air.

Ready? Give your description to Claude Code:

💬 Say this to Claude Code
Use Meshy to generate a 3D model preview from this description:

"[YOUR DESCRIPTION]"

When it's done, show me the preview image. Then download the 3D model and create an HTML viewer I can open in my browser to spin it around.
Replace [YOUR DESCRIPTION] with what you wrote on paper. Keep it under 500 characters — be dense with detail, not wordy. This is a preview — no textures yet, just the shape. You'll refine it later if the shape is right.

The wait. The preview takes about 60-70 seconds. While you wait:

While you wait
Write down what you THINK will appear vs. what you actually DESCRIBED. When the model arrives, compare. The gap between what you imagined and what you described — that's what your next prompt will close.

When it arrives, open the 3D viewer. Rotate it. Zoom in. Look at it from every angle. Is the shape right? Don't worry about color or texture yet — the preview is just the raw geometry.

💡
Look at the bottom. Flip the model upside down in the viewer. Is there a flat base? If the bottom is open or uneven, add "sitting on a solid display base" to your next prompt. You need a flat bottom for 3D printing.

If the shape looks good, tell Claude Code to refine it:

💬 Say this to Claude Code
The shape looks good. Refine this model for better quality and textures.
Only refine if you're happy with the shape. If it's wrong, skip to Mission 2 and rewrite your description instead — don't waste a refine on a bad shape.

Look at your first model. What's right? What's missing? What surprised you?

Here's the thing about this AI: you cannot tweak. You can't say "make the horns bigger" or "add more detail to the left side." It generates from scratch every time. So iteration means rewriting your entire description, informed by what you learned from the first one.

This is a different skill than what you used in Days 4-5, where you changed one thing at a time. Here, you rewrite the whole brief. Keep what worked. Change what didn't. Add what was missing.

Before your second prompt
Look at your first model and answer:

→ What details appeared that you DID describe? (keep those words)
→ What's missing that you FORGOT to describe? (add those words)
→ What appeared that you DIDN'T want? (add a note: "no [thing]")
→ Is the overall style right? (realistic, stylized, cartoon?)
💬 Say this to Claude Code
I want to try a new version. Use Meshy to generate a 3D model preview from this improved description:

"[YOUR IMPROVED DESCRIPTION]"

When it's done, show me the preview and create a viewer. I want to compare this one with my first version.
💡
If your first model is exactly what you wanted — skip this mission and go straight to Mission 3. But most of the time, the second prompt is better. Now you know what the AI does with your words.
// example — prompt iteration
First prompt

"A dragon sitting on a rock, with wings and horns. Detailed scales. On a base."

Result: Dragon is there, but the wings are spread wide (hard to print), the base is paper-thin, and it looks like a cartoon.

Second prompt (improved)

"A fierce dragon with wings folded tight against its body, coiled around a tall rocky outcrop. Heavy scales with scratches and battle scars. Two swept-back horns. Thick muscular tail wrapped around the base. Realistic style, dark and weathered. Solid single piece on a thick stone pedestal."

What changed: "Wings folded tight" solves the printing overhang. "Coiled around a rocky outcrop" gives it structure instead of thin legs in the air. "Thick stone pedestal" replaces the flimsy base. Added "realistic style" to kill the cartoon look. "Solid single piece" for printability.

  1. Study your first model. Identify what worked and what's missing.
  2. Rewrite the full description on paper. Keep the good parts, fix the rest.
  3. Generate a new preview. Compare the two shapes in the viewers.
  4. Pick your favorite. Refine that one for full quality.

Your model exists on screen. It has hundreds of thousands of tiny triangles — beautiful for rendering, but your printer and slicer need a leaner file. Claude Code is going to optimize it for the physical world: shrink the triangle count, scale it to the right size, fix any geometry problems, and set up your slicer.

💬 Say this to Claude Code
I want to 3D print my best model on my Qidi Q1 Pro. Here's what I need:

1. Use Meshy's remesh tool to convert it to STL format, reduce it to about 30,000 triangles, and scale it to [SIZE] cm tall. Set the origin to the bottom so it sits flat.
2. Download the print-ready STL file.
3. Check the mesh: is it watertight? Any problems?
4. Tell me the slicer settings I should use: layer height, walls, infill, supports, brim. And the estimated print time.
5. Give me step-by-step instructions for loading it into Qidi Studio.
Replace [SIZE] with the height you want from the size chart above. 10 cm is a good starting point.

Claude Code will come back with a report. The key things to check:

  • 📈 Triangle count: Raw Meshy models have around 600K triangles. After optimization, around 30K. Your printer can't resolve detail finer than ~0.4mm anyway — extra triangles just slow down the slicer.
  • ✅ Watertight: Should say "yes" or "repaired." If it says the mesh can't be fixed, see the troubleshooting panel below.
  • 📏 Supports: Models with overhangs (arms reaching out, horns at angles) will need support material. Claude Code will tell you. It adds print time but prevents droopy plastic.
  • ⏱ Print time: A 10 cm model is typically 3-5 hours depending on complexity and supports. Check that the estimate seems right for your size.

Load it into the slicer and start the print. Follow Claude Code's step-by-step instructions for Qidi Studio. Watch the first few layers — if the base sticks well, you're good. Then go do something else while the printer works.

// what if something goes wrong
Mesh can't be repaired

Some Meshy models have geometry too broken to auto-fix. Go back to Mission 1 or 2 and add these words to your prompt: "solid single piece, thick walls, no thin floating details, sitting on a solid base." Regenerate.

Slicer shows red areas

Red areas are unsupported overhangs. Tell Claude Code: "The slicer shows unsupported areas. Should I enable supports, or can you orient the model differently?" Claude Code will figure out the best approach.

Thin details break during printing

Horn tips, antenna, thin sword blades — these are fragile in PLA. If they break off, glue them back with super glue. For next time, mention "sturdy proportions" or "thick details" in your prompt.

Model has no flat bottom

Tell Claude Code: "Add a simple flat circular base to the bottom of this model, about 5mm thick, so it sits flat on the print bed." Or regenerate with "sitting on a solid display base" in your prompt.
