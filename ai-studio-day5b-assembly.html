The Assembly Rule

Test the joint first. The weakest point in any multi-part build is where the pieces meet. Before you build every piece, validate just the joint area — a small chunk of each side where they connect. Render it from multiple angles, check the exploded view, verify dimensions in code. If it doesn't fit, you fix the design in seconds. Changing code is free. Reprinting costs time and filament.

I want to build a 3D-printable object made of multiple pieces that connect together. Before we start, research how multi-part 3D prints work: what types of joints are used to connect printed pieces (tabs and slots, pegs and holes, snap fits, flat glue surfaces), how to design joints that actually fit when printed, and how to split a model into pieces that each print on their own. Tell me the key things I should understand.

Claude Code will come back with a summary. The most important things to watch for:

  • 🔌 Joint types: Tabs that slide into slots, pegs that fit into holes, flat surfaces for gluing. Each works differently — the right choice depends on your object.
  • 📏 Each piece prints alone: Every piece needs its own flat base, watertight mesh, and no supports — same rules as Day 5, applied to each piece individually.
  • 🔄 Direction matters: The pieces need to physically come together from some direction — slide in, push down, snap on. The joint has to be designed for how the pieces actually meet.
💡
Same technique, deeper knowledge. You used "teach the machine" in Day 5 for overhangs and watertight meshes. Now you're adding joint design to Claude Code's knowledge. Each time you do this, the AI builds smarter.
🔨
New tool: OpenSCAD. This session uses a code-based modeling tool called OpenSCAD for building geometry. Every shape, every joint, every dimension is defined in code — Claude Code writes it, you direct it. The advantage: joints are precise, dimensions are exact, and every piece is guaranteed solid and watertight. When you need a 5mm tab fitting into a 5.2mm slot, the code says exactly that.

You'll still see your models in an interactive 3D viewer you can rotate — same as Day 5. The difference is what's under the hood: OpenSCAD geometry instead of hand-built shapes.
  1. Open Claude Code and give it the research prompt above.
  2. Read what it finds. Pay attention to how different joint types work — you'll pick one in the next mission.
  3. Ask any questions. "What joint would work best for connecting a blade to a handle?" is a good one.

Write Your Split Plan
Answer these four questions:

→ What is the full object? (name and description)
→ Where does it split? (which pieces, and why split there)
→ How do the pieces connect? (joint type for each connection)
→ How does each piece print? (flat base, orientation, any tricky overhangs)
✅
Split at natural boundaries. A sword splits at the guard — blade on one side, handle on the other. A helmet splits at the jawline. Don't split through the middle of a detail. Split where it makes physical sense, and where each resulting piece can sit flat on the print bed.
// example — split plans
Katana — 3 pieces

Full object: A katana with curved blade, circular guard (tsuba), and wrapped handle.

Split: Three pieces — blade, guard, and handle. Split at the guard, which is the natural boundary.

Joints: The blade has a rectangular tab on the end that slides through a matching slot in the guard. The handle has a rectangular tab that slides into the guard from the other side. Both tabs are slightly tapered so they wedge in tight.

Printing: Blade prints on its side (flat edge down). Guard prints flat. Handle prints standing up on its wide end.

Viking Shield — 3 pieces

Full object: A round Viking shield, bigger than a single print — 200mm diameter, with a metal boss in the center and a grip behind it.

Split: Two shield halves (split down the middle) plus the center boss as a separate piece.

Joints: The two halves have interlocking tabs along the split line — three tabs on one side, three matching slots on the other. They slide together left-to-right. The boss has a circular peg that pushes into a matching hole where the halves meet.

Printing: Each half prints face-down (flat back on the bed). The boss prints dome-side-up on a flat base.

Notice: Every split plan describes the direction pieces come together, and every piece has a flat surface for the print bed.

Phase 03 — Test

Build the Pieces
20 min
›

This is the mission that saves you from wasting hours. Before building the full object, you build just the joint area in OpenSCAD — a small chunk of each side where the pieces connect. Then you view it in an interactive 3D viewer, spin it around, and check that everything fits. If it doesn't, you fix the code in seconds.

💬 Say this to Claude Code
Build me OpenSCAD models for a test piece of the connection between [PIECE A] and [PIECE B] — just the joint area, about 2-3cm of material on each side. Export the pieces as STL files, then create an HTML viewer I can open in my browser that shows both pieces. Give me a slider or button to toggle between assembled (pieces together) and exploded (pieces pulled apart) views. Color each piece differently so I can tell them apart.
Replace [PIECE A] and [PIECE B] with the pieces from your split plan. If you have multiple joints, start with the trickiest one.

Open the HTML viewer in your browser. Drag to rotate, scroll to zoom — same controls as Day 5. Use the explode toggle to pull the pieces apart and push them back together. Look at the joint from every angle:

  • 🔌 Assembled view: Do the pieces meet cleanly? No gaps, no overlaps?
  • 🔄 Exploded view: Can you see the matching shapes — tab on one side, slot on the other?
  • 📏 Connection direction: Could you actually slide these together with your hands?

If something looks off, tell Claude Code exactly what's wrong. It updates the OpenSCAD code, re-exports, and refreshes the viewer — iterate until it's right.

📐
What to check in the viewer: Do the joint surfaces match exactly? Is the tab slightly smaller than the slot (it should be — you need clearance for printing)? Are there any thin walls or overhangs in the joint area? Zoom in on the joint area. If anything looks wrong, tell Claude Code what to fix.
💡
You're validating digitally before committing to a print. If the joint looks wrong in the render, fix it now — changing code is free, reprinting costs time and filament.
// example — testing a katana guard joint
Round 1 — Build & View

You: Build me OpenSCAD test pieces for the connection between the blade and the guard. Just the last 3cm of the blade (with the rectangular tab) and the guard (with the matching slot). Export as STL and give me an HTML viewer with an explode toggle. Color them differently.

Claude Code: [writes .scad file, exports STLs, creates HTML viewer — blade piece in silver, guard piece in bronze, explode slider at the bottom]

Round 2 — Spin & Spot Problems

You: [opens viewer, rotates the pieces, toggles explode on and off] The tab is round but the slot is square — they don't match. And the tab looks way too small, it would rattle around in there.

Claude Code: Good catch. I'll make the tab rectangular to match the slot, and increase it to leave only 0.2mm clearance per side.

Round 3 — Fix & Refresh

You: Yes, rectangular tab, 0.2mm clearance. Update the viewer.

Claude Code: [updates .scad, re-exports, refreshes viewer — tab and slot match, snug fit]

You: [spins it around, toggles explode — looks solid from every angle]

Total time: ~5 minutes. Three iterations, zero filament. The joint is validated before you ever print.

  1. Tell Claude Code to build OpenSCAD test pieces for your trickiest joint and create an HTML viewer.
  2. Open the viewer in your browser. Spin the pieces around. Toggle explode on and off.
  3. Check: does the tab match the slot? Is the fit snug? Does the connection direction make sense?
  4. If anything looks wrong, tell Claude Code exactly what's off. It fixes the code and updates the viewer.
  5. Keep iterating until the joint looks right from every angle.
🏆
This is how real engineers work. In product design and manufacturing, you always validate the hard parts first. Nobody builds a whole product and then discovers the hinge doesn't work. You test the hinge digitally, then build the product.

This is your last chance to catch problems before committing to a print. Toggle the explode slider — every joint should show matching shapes. In assembled view, the full object should look complete with no gaps. If analyze_model reports any issues, fix them now.

💬 Export for printing
Export each piece as its own STL file, named [project]-[piece].stl. These need to be ready for slicing.
  1. Build each piece one at a time in OpenSCAD. Review each in the 3D viewer before moving on.

  4. Export each piece as its own STL file. One file per piece, named clearly.
